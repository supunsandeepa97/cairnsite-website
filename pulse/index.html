Use the exact same name, address and phone everywhere online

Before search engines show a business prominently in local results, they try to confirm it's real and trustworthy — and one quiet signal they weigh is consistency. If your business name, address and phone number appear slightly differently across the web (one listing says "St", another "Street"; one shows an old number), it muddies the picture and can quietly hold you back.

The fix is simple and free: decide on one exact way to write your name, address and phone, then make every listing match it — your website, Google Business Profile, Facebook, Instagram and any directories you appear in. Use the number people actually reach you on. When you move, change numbers or rebrand, update them all together rather than one at a time. A clean, matching set of details makes you easier for both people and search engines to trust — and a website you control gives you one reliable source of truth to copy everywhere else.

Claim your free Google Business Profile — it's the fastest way to show up on Google Maps

When someone searches for a business like yours nearby, Google often shows a map with a short list of local options before anything else. To appear there, you need a Google Business Profile — and setting one up costs nothing. Search for "Google Business Profile", sign in, and either claim your existing listing or create a new one.

Fill in the essentials: your exact business name, address (or service area), phone number, opening hours and website. Pick the most accurate category, since that's a big part of how Google decides when to show you. Add a few good photos and a short description while you're there. Google will usually ask to verify you're really the owner, so follow the prompts. Once you're live and verified, you become far easier to find — and a simple website gives that listing somewhere solid to point to.

The 3 photos every local business should have online

People decide whether to trust you in seconds, and photos do a lot of that work. You don't need a professional shoot — a clean, well-lit phone photo is plenty. Focus on three shots that answer the questions every new customer quietly asks.

First, your storefront or entrance, so people recognise the place when they arrive. Second, your product or service in action — the dish you're known for, a finished job, a tidy treatment room — so they can picture what they'll actually get. Third, you or your team, because a friendly face turns a faceless listing into real people worth visiting. Take them in daylight, keep the background uncluttered, and use them everywhere: your website, your Google listing and your social pages. Consistent, honest photos quietly reassure customers before they ever message you.

What to put on your website's contact section

Plenty of customers are ready to reach you — they just give up when it takes effort to find out how. Your contact section should make getting in touch feel effortless, with everything visible at a glance and nothing buried in a paragraph.

Include a tappable phone number, a WhatsApp link so people can message in one tap, your opening hours, and a map link or address so they know exactly where to go. If you prefer email, add that too. The trick is to make each item clickable on a phone: tapping the number should start a call, tapping the address should open maps. Put this section somewhere obvious — near the top and again in the footer — so no one has to hunt for it. A website you control lets you lay all this out clearly, instead of hoping people dig through a social profile.

Keep your opening hours online and correct

Nothing loses a customer faster than wrong hours. Someone drives over on the strength of what they saw online, finds a locked door, and that's a sale gone — plus a fair chance they won't try again. Accurate hours are one of the simplest trust signals you can offer.

Make a habit of checking your hours in every place they appear: your website, your Google Business Profile and your social pages. They should all match. The moments that catch people out are the predictable ones — public holidays, seasonal changes, a day you close early. Update those a little ahead of time rather than after the fact. If your schedule shifts often, say so plainly ("hours can vary — message us to confirm") and point people to your WhatsApp so they can check in seconds. A few minutes of upkeep keeps customers turning up when you're actually open.

A simple, polite way to ask happy customers for a review

Reviews do two jobs at once: they help new customers trust you, and they help you get found, since listings with steady, genuine reviews tend to stand out more in local search results. The good news is that most happy customers are glad to leave one — they simply need to be asked, and to be shown how.

The best moment is right after a good experience, while it's fresh. Keep it warm and low-pressure: "I'm really glad you're happy with it — if you have a minute, a quick review would mean a lot and helps other people find us." Then make it easy by sending a direct link to your Google review page, ideally over WhatsApp so it's one tap away. Never offer anything in exchange for a review, and always reply to the ones you get — a short, friendly thank-you shows future customers there's a real, attentive person behind the business.
